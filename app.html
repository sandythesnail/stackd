Sources & References

Specific facts, rates, and figures used across the modules, and where they come from.
Earning
  1. FICA payroll tax rate - 7.65% total (6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare). Internal Revenue Service, "Topic no. 751, Social Security and Medicare withholding rates." irs.gov
  2. Work-Study earnings are generally exempt from FICA tax while a student is enrolled and regularly attending classes at least half-time. Internal Revenue Service, "Student FICA exception" (26 U.S.C. §3121(b)(10)). irs.gov
  3. A payer must send a 1099-NEC once payments to a contractor reach $2,000 in a calendar year - up from the earlier $600 threshold as of 2026; all income is reportable regardless of whether a form was sent. Internal Revenue Service, "Reporting payments to independent contractors." irs.gov
  4. Self-employed individuals generally must make quarterly estimated tax payments if they expect to owe $1,000 or more for the year. Internal Revenue Service, "Self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare taxes)." irs.gov
Spending
  1. The FAFSA opens every year on October 1st. Federal Student Aid, "FAFSA Deadlines." studentaid.gov
  2. A full emergency fund covers roughly 3-6 months of expenses. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "An essential guide to building an emergency fund." consumerfinance.gov
  3. The 50/30/20 budget rule splits after-tax income into roughly 50% needs, 30% wants, and 20% savings/debt repayment. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Money as You Grow: Analyzing budgets." consumerfinance.gov
  4. The FAFSA uses "prior-prior year" tax information - the 2026-27 FAFSA relies on 2024 tax data, not the most recently filed return. Federal Student Aid, "Why do I have to submit my 2024 tax and income information for the 2026-27 FAFSA form?" studentaid.gov
Saving
  1. FDIC insurance covers deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. FDIC, "Understanding Deposit Insurance." fdic.gov
  2. High-yield savings APYs vs. the near-zero rate on a typical traditional savings account - actual rates move with the market; check current national averages. FDIC, "National Rates and Rate Caps." fdic.gov
  3. Overdraft fees commonly run $30+ per incident. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Data Spotlight: Consumer experiences with overdraft programs." consumerfinance.gov
  4. A full emergency fund covers roughly 3-6 months of expenses. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "An essential guide to building an emergency fund." consumerfinance.gov
  5. A $500 starter emergency fund is a commonly recommended first target before working toward the full 3-6 month goal. NerdWallet, "Emergency Fund: What it Is and Why it Matters." nerdwallet.com
  6. Which banks lead on high-yield savings APYs changes often with the rate environment - check current listings rather than a fixed number or bank name. NerdWallet, "Best High-Yield Savings Accounts." nerdwallet.com
Investing
  1. Roth IRA annual contribution limit set by the IRS. Internal Revenue Service, "Retirement topics - IRA contribution limits." irs.gov
  2. Roth IRA eligibility is based on having earned income, not age or job type. Internal Revenue Service, Publication 590-A. irs.gov
  3. Long-run stock market returns average roughly 7%/year after inflation - a commonly-cited historical average that varies by source, time period, and methodology. "S&P 500 Historical Annual Returns (1927-2026)." macrotrends.net
  4. Major brokerages including Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard offer $0 account minimums and $0 commission on stock/ETF trades. Fidelity, "Straightforward and Transparent Pricing." fidelity.com
  5. Withdrawing Roth IRA/401(k) earnings before age 59½ generally triggers a 10% additional tax, though withdrawing your own Roth contributions does not. Internal Revenue Service, "Retirement topics - Exceptions to tax on early distributions." irs.gov
  6. The "Rule of 72" estimates years to double an investment by dividing 72 by the annual rate of return. U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, Investor.gov compound interest calculator. investor.gov
Managing Credit
  1. FICO scores range 300-850; 670+ is "Good," 740+ is "Very Good," 800+ is "Exceptional." myFICO, "What is a Credit Score?" myfico.com
  2. FICO score factor weights: payment history 35%, amounts owed 30%, length of history 15%, new credit 10%, credit mix 10%. myFICO, "How are FICO Scores Calculated?" myfico.com
  3. The three nationwide credit bureaus are Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion; free reports at AnnualCreditReport.com. FTC, "Free Credit Reports." consumer.ftc.gov
  4. Hard inquiries can stay on a credit report for up to two years. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "What is a credit inquiry?" consumerfinance.gov
  5. Rate-shopping inquiries made within a 14-45 day window count as a single inquiry for scoring purposes. myFICO, "How to Rate Shop and Minimize the Impact to Your FICO Scores." myfico.com
  6. Payday loans commonly carry APRs around 300-400%. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "What is an APR ... for my payday loan?" consumerfinance.gov
  7. Retail/store credit cards commonly carry APRs of 25-30%+, notably higher than general-purpose cards. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Issue Spotlight: The High Cost of Retail Credit Cards." consumerfinance.gov
  8. Balance transfer fees commonly run 3-5% of the amount transferred. Experian, "What Is a Balance Transfer Fee?" experian.com
  9. Secured credit cards typically require a refundable deposit, often starting around $200. Bankrate, "How Much Is A Secured Credit Card Deposit?" bankrate.com
  10. Credit bureaus are generally required to investigate a dispute within 30 days, at no cost to you. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "How long does it take to repair an error on a credit report?" consumerfinance.gov
Managing Risk
  1. Under the ACA, young adults can generally stay on a parent's health plan until age 26. HealthCare.gov, "Health Insurance Coverage For Children and Young Adults Under 26." healthcare.gov
  2. Federal law (Regulation E) caps your liability for unauthorized debit card charges at $50 if reported within 2 business days, $500 if reported later, unlimited after 60 days. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Sec. 1005.6 Liability of consumer for unauthorized transfers." consumerfinance.gov
  3. Renters insurance averages roughly $10-20/month nationally. NerdWallet, "How Much Is Renters Insurance?" nerdwallet.com
  4. Auto and renters/homeowners liability coverage caps out at a maximum payout, often in the $100,000-$300,000 range depending on the policy. Insurance Information Institute, "Renters Insurance." iii.org
  5. Auto liability insurance is legally required in nearly every state. Insurance Information Institute, "Background on: Compulsory Auto/Uninsured Motorists." iii.org
  6. A $1,000,000 umbrella policy commonly costs around $200-$400/year on top of existing coverage. NerdWallet, "Umbrella Insurance: Coverage & How It Works." nerdwallet.com
Loans
  1. The government pays interest on Direct Subsidized Loans while you're enrolled at least half-time; Unsubsidized loans accrue interest from disbursement. Federal Student Aid, "Subsidized and Unsubsidized Loans." studentaid.gov
  2. A 6-month grace period applies after graduating or dropping below half-time enrollment before federal loan payments begin. Federal Student Aid, "How Long Is My Grace Period." studentaid.gov
  3. A Master Promissory Note and entrance counseling are required before your first federal loan disbursement. Federal Student Aid, Master Promissory Note (Direct Subsidized/Unsubsidized). studentaid.gov
  4. Federal loans default to the Standard Repayment Plan - a fixed 10-year schedule for loans first disbursed before July 1, 2026, or a tiered 10-25 year schedule based on balance for loans disbursed on or after that date. Federal Student Aid, "Federal Student Loan Repayment Plans." studentaid.gov
  5. Parent PLUS Loans require a credit check and legally cannot be transferred into the student's name. Federal Student Aid, "Parent PLUS Loans"; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Options for repaying your Parent PLUS loans." studentaid.gov
  6. Half-time enrollment for undergrads is commonly around 6 credits per semester, though each school sets its own exact threshold. Federal Student Aid, "Half-Time Enrollment." studentaid.gov
  7. Starting July 1, 2026, Grad PLUS Loans were discontinued for new borrowers; graduate students now borrow through higher Direct Unsubsidized Loan limits instead ($20,500/year, $100,000 lifetime; $50,000/year, $200,000 lifetime for select professional-degree programs). Federal Student Aid, "Big Updates to Federal Student Aid." studentaid.gov
  8. Interest that accrues during a deferment or forbearance is generally added to (capitalized into) the loan's principal once that period ends. Federal Student Aid, "What is capitalized interest?" studentaid.gov
  9. Enrolling in autopay on a federal student loan generally comes with an interest rate reduction. Federal Student Aid, "Auto Pay Interest Rate Reduction." studentaid.gov
Taxes
  1. The federal individual tax filing deadline is typically April 15th. Internal Revenue Service, "Filing." irs.gov
  2. American Opportunity Tax Credit - up to $2,500/year, up to 40% ($1,000) refundable, first four years of postsecondary education. Internal Revenue Service, "Education credits - AOTC and LLC." irs.gov
  3. Lifetime Learning Credit - up to $2,000/year (20% of up to $10,000 in expenses), no limit on number of years claimed. Internal Revenue Service, "Education credits - AOTC and LLC." irs.gov
  4. Self-employment tax is 15.3%, owed once net self-employment earnings reach $400; estimated quarterly payments are generally required if you expect to owe $1,000+ for the year. Internal Revenue Service, "Self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare taxes)." irs.gov
  5. Form 1098-T reports tuition paid and scholarships/grants received, used to help calculate education credits. Internal Revenue Service, "About Form 1098-T." irs.gov
  6. Combined with direct deposit, the IRS issues most e-filed refunds in less than 21 days. Internal Revenue Service, "Direct deposit fastest way to receive federal tax refund." irs.gov
  7. Quarterly estimated tax payments are generally due in mid-April, June, September, and January. Internal Revenue Service, "Estimated Taxes." irs.gov
  8. A filing extension only extends the deadline to file a return - any tax owed is still due by the original deadline. Internal Revenue Service, "Need more time to file? Don't wait, request an extension." irs.gov
  9. A Form W-4 can be updated with an employer at any time to adjust paycheck withholding. Internal Revenue Service, "About Form W-4." irs.gov
  10. IRS Free File and free VITA tax-prep clinics are legitimate no-cost filing options for qualifying taxpayers. Internal Revenue Service, "IRS Free File." irs.gov
  11. The U.S. uses a progressive, marginal tax bracket system - only income within a given bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. Internal Revenue Service, "Federal income tax rates and brackets." irs.gov
Consumer Psychology
  1. Mental accounting - the tendency to treat money differently based on its source or intended use, even though a dollar spends the same regardless. Richard Thaler, "Mental Accounting Matters" (1999); overview via The Decision Lab. thedecisionlab.com
  2. The sunk cost fallacy - the tendency to keep investing in something because of what's already been spent, rather than its future value. The Decision Lab, "The Sunk Cost Fallacy." thedecisionlab.com
  3. A scarcity mindset - feeling short on money or time narrows attention onto the immediate problem and can worsen decision-making on other tradeoffs. Mullainathan & Shafir, "Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much"; summary via Harvard Kennedy School Center for International Development. hks.harvard.edu
  4. Missed Buy Now, Pay Later payments can, depending on the provider, be reported to credit bureaus and affect a credit score. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, "Buy Now, Pay Later and Credit Reporting." consumerfinance.gov
Career & Salary
  1. Job switchers and job stayers see different wage growth, and the size of that gap shifts with the labor market over time - it's not a fixed, guaranteed spread. Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta, "Wage Growth Tracker." atlantafed.org
  2. Research tracing to sociologist Mark Granovetter's studies on job-seeking puts the share of workers who found their job through a personal contact at roughly 30-60%, notably lower than some inflated career-advice figures. Granovetter, "Getting a Job: A Study of Contacts and Careers"; summarized via NIH Office of Intramural Training & Education, "Networking: The Strength of Weak Ties." training.nih.gov
  3. An employer 401(k) match is often described as one of the closest things to a guaranteed, risk-free return available to an investor. NerdWallet, "What Is a 401(k) Match?" nerdwallet.com
Scams & Fraud Prevention
  1. Credit freezes are free by federal law and must be requested separately with each of the three bureaus. FTC, "Credit Freezes and Fraud Alerts." consumer.ftc.gov
  2. IdentityTheft.gov is the FTC's official site for reporting identity theft and building a personal recovery plan. Federal Trade Commission. identitytheft.gov
  3. .gov domains are restricted to verified government organizations and can't be purchased by anyone else. General Services Administration, "Eligibility for .gov domains." get.gov
  4. P2P payment apps like Venmo, Cash App, and Zelle are built for paying people you already trust, and generally offer no fraud dispute process once a payment is sent, unlike a credit card. FTC, "Do you use payment apps like Venmo, CashApp, or Zelle? Read this." consumer.ftc.gov
  5. A credit card chargeback lets you dispute a charge with your card issuer if an order never arrives or isn't as described. FTC, "Using Credit Cards and Disputing Charges." consumer.ftc.gov
  6. Gift card and wire transfer payments generally can't be reversed once sent, which is why scammers favor them. FTC, "Avoiding and Reporting Gift Card Scams." consumer.ftc.gov
  7. Banks must generally make deposited funds available within a few business days, but a fraudulent check can take weeks to actually bounce, letting fake-check scams look "cleared" before the fraud surfaces. FTC, "How To Spot, Avoid, and Report Fake Check Scams." consumer.ftc.gov
  8. Legitimate scholarships never require paying a fee to receive an award. FTC, "How To Avoid Scholarship and Financial Aid Scams." consumer.ftc.gov
  9. Real companies do not proactively pop up a warning or cold-call telling you to call them about a virus. FTC, "How To Spot, Avoid, and Report Tech Support Scams." consumer.ftc.gov
  10. Romance scams typically ask for money via gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency - payment methods that are hard to trace or reverse. FTC, "What To Know About Romance Scams." consumer.ftc.gov
